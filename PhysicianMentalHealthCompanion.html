1. Executive Summary

The idea is to build an agentic support layer for physicians: a workflow-aware companion that connects calendars, schedules, EHR-derived workload signals, and a trained peer-support network—but only within tight, clinician-approved boundaries. The differentiator is not another meditation app; it is infrastructure that routes doctors to humans who have done this work, plus optional social reconnection after hard shifts.

The product should not be “AI therapist for doctors.” It should be “confidential routing infrastructure after the hardest moments in care”—with peer support, a physician-chosen Circle of Trust, and deliberate encouragement of collegial social time.

Research anchors: Kaiser Permanente’s POST program (306 peer interactions over 3 years; ~49% of physicians engaged); Johns Hopkins RISE; Health Affairs linkage of system-generated in-basket volume to ~40% higher burnout odds; Mayo commensality pilots showing improved emotional exhaustion vs. controls.

2. Product Positioning

Strong positioning

A confidential support router that uses schedule, shift, and event context to detect when a physician may need help, then routes them to self-guided reset, peer support, licensed help, admin relief, or crisis escalation.

Context-aware Peer network first Circle of Trust EHR-aware (metadata) Social collegiality

Weak positioning

A generic wellness chatbot that asks doctors how they feel, offers meditation content, or claims to provide therapy through AI. This would be easy to ignore and risky to deploy.

Not AI therapy Not surveillance Not generic EAP Not EHR automation
Best one-line pitch: “The confidential support layer that routes physicians to peers, trusted colleagues, and recovery—triggered by clinical context, not generic wellness check-ins.”

3. How It Works

1
Read low-risk context first

Connect to calendar, call schedule, shift schedule, appointment volume, missed breaks, after-hours work, and self-reported hard events. Avoid full patient-chart ingestion in the MVP.

2
Detect high-stress moments

Identify patterns such as a patient death, surgical complication, night call, litigation threat, hostile encounter, excessive charting, or repeated intense shifts.

3
Offer a tiny, useful choice

The interface should offer three clear actions: reset now, talk to someone, or handle admin. No long forms, no content library, no generic check-in.

4
Route to peer support first

Match to a trained peer physician (hospital roster or specialty pool), offer a Circle-of-Trust outreach option, schedule licensed care, or trigger crisis protocol. Third-party referrals—colleague, charge nurse, APP—should be supported like POST programs do.

5
Circle of Trust (opt-in, physician-controlled)

Each doctor curates 3–7 trusted colleagues (spouse, mentor, co-resident, friend-physician). If multiple stress signals accumulate within a window the physician configured, the app asks: “Reach out to your circle?” Nothing is sent without explicit approval in that moment—except pre-authorized crisis pathways.

6
Encourage social recovery

Suggest protected collegial time: walk with a peer, shared meal, brief debrief coffee, specialty huddle—not “another webinar.” Evidence supports commensality and small-group collegiality for burnout domains.

7
Report only aggregate trends

Hospitals receive department-level risk patterns, never individual emotional logs or Circle-of-Trust activity. Trust is the product.

5. What It Should Not Do

Do not allow Reason
Autonomous patient-specific medical advice This moves the product into clinical decision support and creates regulatory and liability risk.
Changing orders or documenting in the EHR without review Physician approval must remain mandatory for any clinical action.
Notifying leadership that “Dr. X is burned out” This destroys trust and creates employment, licensing, and credentialing fears.
Storing identifiable patient stories unnecessarily PHI exposure should be avoided in the MVP and minimized later.
Diagnosing the physician The system should detect support needs, not label doctors with psychiatric conditions.
Alerting a Circle of Trust without live physician consent Even well-meaning “concern” messages can harm careers and trust. Multi-signal detection should surface a prompt to the physician first; outreach sends only after approval (crisis carve-outs excepted).
Employer visibility into who is in whose circle Circle membership and alert history are clinician-owned data, not HR analytics.

7. Peer Support Network (Core Pillar)

Peer support is the product’s human backbone—not an afterthought to AI debrief. Hospital programs like Kaiser Permanente’s POST and Johns Hopkins RISE show that physicians accept help from trained peers more readily than from generic EAP or leadership-driven wellness.

What works in published programs

  • Physician-only peers with 4+ hours of training on confidentiality, red flags, and when to escalate
  • Department-embedded supporters who understand local culture (surgery vs. OB vs. ED)
  • Third-party referrals—colleagues can flag concern without exposing the distressed physician to HR
  • 48-hour outreach SLA from referral to peer contact (POST norm)
  • Median ~60-minute conversations—substantive, not a checkbox
  • Leadership-funded time protection for peer leads and training refreshers

How the platform operationalizes peers

  • Roster of trained supporters with specialty, language, gender preference, and on-call windows
  • Intelligent match after adverse events (complication, death, litigation scare)
  • Secure booking + optional video, with no session notes flowing to employer dashboards
  • Peer workload caps and burnout monitoring for supporters themselves
  • Integration with existing POST/RISE/well-being lead programs rather than replacing them
  • Outcome metrics at program level: reach, time-to-contact, repeat use—never transcript mining

8. Circle of Trust

Physicians should choose their own safety net: a small, private list of people who may be invited to check in when the system sees a pattern of strain—not a single alert, and never without the doctor’s live approval in that moment.

1
Onboarding: build the circle

Physician selects 3–7 contacts (colleagues, friends, partner, mentor). Each accepts invite via secure link. Roles: “can receive gentle check-in” vs. “crisis-only.”

2
Configure thresholds (physician-owned)

Example: “If 3+ support prompts are declined in 7 days AND after-hours charting exceeds my baseline AND I tap ‘hard day’ twice → ask me about alerting my circle.” Physician sets sensitivity; defaults are conservative.

3
Multi-signal detection (no auto-send)

Signals can include: repeated hard-event tags, elevated in-basket/pajama-time (from EHR metadata), missed recovery blocks, crisis-adjacent language in private debrief (never auto-forwarded verbatim).

4
Physician approval gate

UI: “Several stress signals this week. Send a pre-written check-in to Alex and Jordan?” Options: Send / Edit message / Not now / Talk to peer instead. No silent notifications to the circle.

5
What the circle receives

Non-clinical, non-diagnostic message: “A colleague asked us to check in—they’ve had a rough stretch. A text or coffee might help.” No patient details, no “burnout score,” no employer CC.

Principle Implementation
Opt-in only Circle feature disabled until physician completes setup
Approval per outreach Multi-signal logic triggers a prompt, not an automatic SMS
Crisis exception Pre-authorized crisis contacts can be called immediately if explicit crisis language detected; separate consent flow at signup
Employer firewall Circle membership and messages excluded from hospital analytics APIs
Revocable One-tap pause or remove any circle member; audit log visible only to physician
This is the anti-surveillance feature: the hospital sees heatmaps; your college roommate sees a text only if you said yes today.

9. EHR Integration Architecture

EHR integration is a phased trust ladder: start with physician-owned context and metadata, not chart mining. Epic (~35%+ U.S. hospital share) and Oracle Health/Cerner expose FHIR R4 via SMART on FHIR; clinician-facing apps launch from Hyperspace/Hyperdrive with OAuth scopes negotiated per hospital.

Phase 0 — No EHR (MVP)

  • Calendar, QGenda/Kronos shift feeds, hard-event button
  • Self-reported events and peer routing
  • Zero PHI in platform storage

Phase 1 — Metadata only

  • SMART launch from Epic Hyperspace (user context, not patient chart)
  • Scopes: openid, fhirUser, Appointment.read, PractitionerRole
  • Signals: appointment volume, session length, after-hours login patterns via EHR analytics exports (e.g., Signal-style aggregates)
  • In-basket message counts—especially system-generated volume (burnout correlate in Health Affairs)

Phase 2 — Event-aware (still minimal PHI)

  • Encounter type flags (death, complication code categories) without storing narrative notes
  • Integration with incident reporting (RLDatix) for post-adverse-event workflows
  • “Debrief now” deep link after closing a traumatic encounter

10. Physician Social Connection & Collegiality

Burnout is not only individual exhaustion—it is erosion of collegiality. Structured social time (commensality, profession-specific small groups) shows measurable gains in emotional exhaustion and workplace community. The product should actively protect and schedule social recovery, not just solo resets.

Evidence-informed tactics

  • Commensality: facilitated shared meals improved emotional exhaustion vs. matched controls (Mayo pilot)
  • Small peer groups: COMPASS-style self-facilitated groups sustained meaning at work for 12+ months
  • Social network interventions: increasing faculty–resident connectivity improved accomplishment and environment scores
  • EHR social graphs: communication patterns with care team predict exhaustion—suggest team huddles or shared breaks when isolation detected

Product features (“social time engine”)

  • Collegiality blocks: 20–45 min holds with suggested peer (same service line)
  • Walk-and-debrief: pair two physicians finishing overlapping OR/ED shifts
  • Commensality mode: group lunch RSVP for department after shared adverse week
  • No-meeting zones: coordinate with chiefs to protect post-call breakfast
  • Residency social graph: nudge PGY peers who haven’t connected in 30 days (opt-in)
  • Celebration prompts: wins matter—successful discharge streak, trainee milestone
“You don’t need another resilience module. You need someone to eat lunch with who gets yesterday’s case.”

Social features must remain voluntary and off employer performance dashboards. Metrics: participation rate, self-reported connection—not minutes logged for HR.

Why TAM is larger than bottom-up SAM at low PMPM

  • Spend pool ≠ SaaS ARR: Hospitals already buy Lyra/Spring, ComPsych EAP, VITAL, well-being indexes, peer programs, and risk/safety modules—your TAM is share of that budget plus net-new post-event infrastructure.
  • Healthcare overweight: HRSA cites ~18M employed in U.S. healthcare (2024)—the largest private employment sector—so a disproportionate slice of the ~$22B U.S. corporate wellness market is healthcare-addressable.
  • Premium clinical pricing: At $50–$100 PMPM (integrated peer + EHR + crisis routing), 18M workers alone implies $10.8B–$21.6B theoretical ARR ceiling—use as upside case, not base SAM.
  • Global: U.S. is ~40% of developed-market opportunity; EU/UK/ANZ add roughly 1.5–2× for global TAM narratives.

15. Trust and Compliance Architecture

Trust requirements

  • Employer never sees individual emotional logs or Circle-of-Trust membership
  • No raw transcripts shared with hospital leadership
  • Circle outreach requires live physician approval (multi-signal → prompt, not auto-alert)
  • Aggregate insights only for hospitals; peer session notes stay peer-owned
  • Clinician controls what gets shared and who is in the circle
  • Clear crisis escalation rules with separate pre-authorized contacts
  • Human-in-the-loop for sensitive actions and EHR writebacks
  • Read-only EHR permissions by default; SMART scopes minimized per App Orchard norms

Compliance watchouts

  • HIPAA and business associate obligations if PHI is processed
  • FDA clinical decision support risk if making patient-care recommendations
  • Employment and credentialing sensitivity
  • Discoverability and legal privilege concerns
  • Security risks from tool permissions and agent actions
  • Clear separation from psychiatric diagnosis claims
The product must be genuinely useful in a physician’s real workflow, but healthcare-grade constrained.

16. Best Initial Wedge

The strongest wedge is post-event support for high-acuity clinicians. This creates urgency and avoids sounding like generic wellness.

Surgery

Support after complications, unexpected outcomes, disclosure conversations, and long operative days.

OB-GYN

Support after fetal loss, maternal complications, emergency deliveries, and litigation-prone events.

Emergency and ICU

Support after trauma, codes, withdrawal-of-care cases, violence, and repeated death exposure.

“Burnout prevention” sounds soft. “Support after the hardest moments in care” sounds necessary.

19. VC Verdict

Question Answer
Is it venture-scale? Yes, if it expands from physicians into clinician workforce infrastructure and risk analytics.
Is the market crowded? Yes around mental health broadly, but less crowded around event-triggered physician support.
What is the wedge? Post-event support for high-acuity clinicians after the hardest moments in care.
What is the moat? Trust architecture (especially Circle of Trust), trained peer network density, EHR-native triggers, collegiality habits, and specialty playbooks.
What kills it? Auto-alerting circles without consent, employer surveillance, generic wellness positioning, or peers treated as unpaid volunteers without protected time.
What makes it a category leader? Becoming the confidential routing layer: EHR context → peer/circle/social recovery → licensed care → aggregate workforce intelligence.
Final thesis: Build a controlled, trusted clinical-workforce companion. It reads workflow context (including EHR metadata when permitted), routes to peers and physician-approved circles, protects collegial social time, and gives hospitals only aggregate risk intelligence—never individual emotional surveillance.
