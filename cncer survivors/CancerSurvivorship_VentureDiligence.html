2) Problem Analysis

Scale of Survivorship

  • US survivors: 18.6M in 2025, projected >22M by 2035 (ACS).
  • US breast survivors: ~4.3M in 2025, projected ~5.3M by 2035 (ACS).
  • Global 5-year prevalence: ~53.5M (IARC GLOBOCAN 2022).

Clinical unmet needs (selected evidence)

  • Fear of recurrence: ~59% moderate FCR; ~19% high/clinical FCR across cancer survivors/patients.
  • Sexual dysfunction:
  • Fatigue:
  • Sleep:

Mental health and psychosocial burden

  • Depression and anxiety remain elevated in survivors versus non-cancer controls.
  • PTSD/stress-related symptoms are present and can persist years post-treatment.

Financial survivorship burden

  • Financial hardship among survivors is common; studies report substantial material and psychological burden.
  • Work disruption is meaningful: ~41% of employed survivors report at least one cancer-related employment change.

Why survivorship remains fragmented

  • SCPs are mandated/encouraged but evidence of outcome improvement is weak in multiple systematic reviews.
  • SCP implementation is resource-heavy and not consistently reimbursed as a dedicated standalone service.
  • Oncology teams are throughput-optimized for treatment, not longitudinal survivorship behavior change.

Special deep dives (requested)

OncoveryCare: clear signal of market validation: survivorship-specific clinical model, oncology anchor partner, and active expansion in community oncology footprint.

Pathway.care: evidence quality is weak/ambiguous. Publicly discoverable data points to patientpathway.care (a nonprofit CANCER101 navigation resource), while "Pathway" search results often refer to unrelated clinical-reference or precision-medicine startups. Investment implication: do not anchor strategy on unclear competitor intelligence; verify legal entity and cap table directly.

Pivot pattern across category: survivorship-only products often drift toward broader navigation, employer channels, or risk-based oncology operations because pure survivorship support monetization is difficult at scale.

5) Why Now (Bull and Bear)

Bull case

  • Survivor population is large and rising.
  • Value-based oncology models fund between-visit services.
  • Women's health investment momentum enables menopause and sexual-health wedges.
  • AI allows low-friction personalization and triage at scale.
  • Provider burnout and workforce shortages increase appetite for outsourced survivorship operations.

Bear case

  • Not every survivor qualifies for every code (CCM needs 2+ conditions; PIN/CCM consent; no minute double-counting).
  • Oncology practices prioritize active-treatment economics before survivorship optimization.
  • Generic navigation incumbents can add survivorship modules quickly.
  • DTC retention can be weak once acute post-treatment fear window passes.
  • Clinical heterogeneity across tumor types can break product simplicity.

7) Product Strategy

Best MVP wedge

  • Breast survivorship track focused on endocrine side effects, menopausal symptoms, sexual/vaginal health, sleep/fatigue, fear of recurrence.
  • Structured weekly ePRO capture + risk stratification + escalation playbooks.
  • Nurse/coach escalation layer with oncology clinical governance.
  • Survivorship care-plan execution tracking (not just document generation).

Do NOT build first

  • Broad cross-cancer marketplace.
  • Beauty and commerce heavy features.
  • Standalone social community as core moat.
  • Deep EMR integrations before proving engagement/outcomes in lightweight integration environments.

AI defensibility opportunities

  • Personalized survivorship trajectories from longitudinal symptom-response data.
  • Protocol-aware recommendation engine tied to NCCN-informed survivorship domains.
  • Risk flags for dropout, unmanaged toxicity, and escalating distress.

AI is not moat by itself; proprietary longitudinal survivorship outcome data + embedded workflows are the moat candidates.

8) Clinical and Regulatory

  • FDA boundary: keep scope in coaching/navigation/supportive care. Avoid diagnostic or treatment recommendation claims that trigger SaMD risk.
  • HIPAA: assume HIPAA-grade architecture if integrated with provider workflows or claims data.
  • Liability: clear escalation protocols for severe mental distress, suicidality, and high-risk symptom reports.
  • NCCN alignment: directly map product domains to survivorship guideline areas (sexual health, menopausal symptoms, psychosocial distress, fatigue, sleep, care coordination).
  • Interoperability: phase integrations: (1) no-EMR intake + portal upload, (2) scheduling/labs/med list interfaces, (3) deeper FHIR events where ROI justifies.

9) Go-To-Market

Best beachhead

Breast cancer survivors age 35-60 in community oncology networks with existing value-based contracts and high endocrine therapy volumes.

Buying committee

  • Economic buyer: practice CEO/CFO or payer medical leader (for risk contracts).
  • Clinical champion: breast oncologist + survivorship NP lead.
  • Operational owner: navigation director/care management lead.

Pilot design

  • 6-month pilot, 300-800 survivors across 2-4 sites.
  • Primary metrics: engagement, symptom burden trajectory, ED/inpatient utilization deltas, completion of key survivorship interventions.
  • Secondary metrics: fear-of-recurrence, sleep/fatigue score changes, return-to-work support utilization.

CAC reality: low consumer CAC is unlikely; distribution must piggyback on existing patient flow via oncology partners or payer navigation channels.

12) Case Studies and Lessons

  • OncoveryCare: validates survivorship care gap and provider willingness to adopt when model is clinical and integrated with oncology operations.
  • Navigating Cancer acquisition: indicates strategic value of oncology workflow and patient engagement infrastructure more than standalone wellness products.
  • Thyme Care trajectory: capital flowed to organizations tied to payer-provider incentives and total cost accountability, not narrow content apps.
  • Midi/Maven analog: women-focused health can scale when payer/employer distribution, insurance coverage, and clinical pathways are strong.
  • Pathway.care ambiguity: unclear competitor identity itself is a diligence warning; avoid narrative risk by verifying entity-level evidence before competitive claims.

13) Final Recommendation

Verdict: Build differently. This can be venture-scale if built as survivorship workflow infrastructure with measurable outcomes, not as a broad consumer survivorship app.

Recommended wedge: endocrine/menopause/sexual-health/FCR program for breast survivors in oncology practice channels.

Recommended model: B2B2C through oncology groups and risk-bearing payers; DTC as supporting intake/engagement layer only.

30-day validation plan:

  • Interview 20 survivors, 20 oncology leaders, 20 payer/employer stakeholders.
  • Prototype one protocolized care pathway and test usability + willingness-to-refer.
  • Model economic impact with partner baseline data (ED, admissions, symptom burden).
  • Secure one design-partner LOI with defined pilot success metrics.
