01Executive Summary

Cancer survivorship is medicine's most neglected afterthought. The U.S. has 18.1 million cancer survivors — a population larger than the entire state of New York — and the number grows by over one million per year. Yet when active treatment ends, the vast majority of survivors fall off a "survivorship cliff": regular contact with their oncology team drops sharply, structured support disappears, and survivors are left to navigate years of ongoing physical, psychological, financial, and social consequences largely alone.

The clinical evidence is unambiguous: depression and anxiety affect 20–40% of survivors at 2–3× the general population rate; fear of recurrence is clinically significant in one-third of all survivors; cancer-related fatigue persists in 30–40% beyond one year post-treatment; and financial toxicity — catastrophic medical debt, life savings depletion, skipped medications — affects nearly half of all patients. These are not rare edge cases. They are the median experience of cancer survivorship in America today.

This report evaluates the opportunity for a context-aware, privacy-first digital companion built specifically for cancer survivors — starting with breast cancer as the optimal entry wedge and expanding systematically to all cancer types.

Who Are Cancer Survivors?

The NCI defines a cancer survivor as "anyone living with, through, or beyond cancer" — from the moment of diagnosis. This means survivorship care begins not at the end of treatment but at diagnosis, and it continues for the rest of a person's life. The needs of a woman three weeks post-mastectomy, a man five years post-prostate radiation, and a young woman 15 years post-childhood leukemia are profoundly different — and all of them are underserved.

The Survivorship Care Plan (SCP) Compliance Gap

In 2005, the Institute of Medicine landmark report From Cancer Patient to Cancer Survivor: Lost in Transition called for every cancer survivor to receive a formal Survivorship Care Plan — a document summarizing treatment, late effects, recommended follow-up, and health guidance. ASCO and the Commission on Cancer now mandate SCPs for accredited programs. Yet two decades later:

  • Only 30–40% of oncology practices have formal, structured survivorship programs[8]
  • An estimated 60% of survivors never receive their SCP, or receive one so late it provides little practical value[8]
  • Of those who do receive an SCP, many report it is too technical, too dense, and not connected to any ongoing support infrastructure[9]
  • Primary care physicians — who inherit survivorship monitoring responsibility — often do not receive the SCP at all, creating a dangerous coordination gap
The SCP mandate creates a massive institutional compliance problem with no adequate digital solution. A platform that generates, delivers, and activates living survivorship care plans becomes essential infrastructure for any oncology practice trying to meet accreditation standards.

The Employer Cost Case

Approximately 1 in 16 working Americans is a cancer survivor — meaning every large employer has a meaningful cancer survivor population on their benefits rolls.[13] Cancer is among the top 5 most expensive conditions for employer health plans. The business case for employer-sponsored survivorship support:

  • Return-to-work: Survivors with active psychosocial support return to work faster and with fewer absences. Studies show structured survivorship programs reduce days off work by 20–40%.[13]
  • Productivity: Cognitive impairment ("chemo brain") affects 17–75% of chemotherapy-treated survivors — directly impairing work performance. Unaddressed, it drives quiet attrition.
  • Benefits claim reduction: Survivors with active management programs use 15–25% fewer high-cost services in years 2–5 post-treatment.[12]
  • Retention: Cancer survivors who feel supported by their employer are significantly more likely to remain employed through and after treatment.
The business case in one line: A platform priced at $25–$75 PMPM for a survivor delivers a calculable ROI to payers (ED avoidance), pharma (adherence revenue and outcomes data), oncology practices (SCP compliance, quality metrics), and employers (productivity, retention) — simultaneously. This multi-buyer structure is rare in digital health and makes the business model far more resilient than single-payer models.

04Clinical Framework — Ten Distinct Survivorship Constructs

Cancer survivorship is not a single clinical problem. It is a constellation of distinct, overlapping, and sometimes interacting conditions — each requiring different interventions, support modalities, and clinical pathways. A generic mental health app cannot address these; a clinically credible platform must distinguish between them and route to appropriate responses.

😰 Fear of Recurrence (FCR)

The fear, worry, or concern about cancer returning or progressing. The most prevalent unmet psychosocial need in survivorship — affecting 70% of survivors, with 33% at clinically significant levels. FCR peaks around surveillance appointments ("scanxiety"), anniversaries of diagnosis, and new physical symptoms.

Evidence-based intervention: CBT-based interventions (CONQUER, Conquering Cancer-Related Worry) show significant FCR reduction. App delivery has been validated.

🧠 Cancer-Related Cognitive Impairment ("Chemo Brain")

Deficits in memory, attention, processing speed, and executive function following cancer treatment. Affects 17–75% of chemotherapy-treated survivors; persists at clinically significant levels in 10–40% at 6+ months post-treatment. Frequently dismissed by clinicians despite major impact on work and daily functioning.

Validated by neuropsychological testing. Cognitive rehabilitation programs show benefit. Peer validation ("I'm not imagining this") is therapeutically significant.

😓 Cancer-Related Fatigue (CRF)

A persistent, distressing sense of physical, emotional, or cognitive tiredness related to cancer or its treatment, not proportional to recent activity and not relieved by rest. The most common cancer symptom — affects 75–80% during treatment and 30–40% at 1+ year post-treatment. Severely impacts quality of life and return to work.

Evidence-based interventions: exercise (aerobic + resistance), behavioral therapy, sleep hygiene, psychostimulants in select cases.

💔 Post-Traumatic Stress & Adjustment Disorder

Cancer diagnosis and treatment are traumatic events. PTSD criteria are met in 5–30% of survivors depending on cancer type and assessment timing; adjustment disorder is substantially more common. Intrusive thoughts, hypervigilance around symptoms, avoidance behaviors, and emotional numbing are common presentations.

Particularly prevalent in breast cancer, head and neck cancer, and those who experienced intensive treatment (transplant, ICU-level care).

💸 Financial Toxicity

The financial burden of cancer and its treatment, including direct costs (copays, medications, travel) and indirect costs (lost income, career disruption). Now recognized as a clinical construct with validated measurement (COST tool). Financial distress directly causes medication non-adherence, skipped follow-up appointments, and delayed symptom reporting.

42% of patients deplete life savings within 2 years. Financial navigation is a clinical intervention, not a social services afterthought.[10]

🌡 Treatment Late Effects & Chronic Symptoms

Persistent or emerging physical effects of cancer treatment that can appear months to years after therapy ends. Varies significantly by cancer type and treatment modality. Examples: lymphedema (breast), neuropathy (chemotherapy), cardiac toxicity (anthracyclines, radiation), bone density loss (aromatase inhibitors), bowel dysfunction (colorectal/pelvic radiation), and secondary malignancies.

Self-monitoring, lifestyle modification, and early escalation to clinical care are critical for managing late effects before they become acute crises.

🫀 Sexual Health & Intimacy Disruption

One of the most prevalent and least addressed survivorship issues. Affects 40–80% of cancer survivors depending on cancer type and treatment. Breast cancer survivors on endocrine therapy: vaginal dryness, dyspareunia, reduced libido. Prostate cancer survivors: erectile dysfunction, ejaculatory changes. Cervical/gynecologic cancer: physical structural changes, body image.

Taboo in most oncology practices. Rarely proactively addressed. Survivors frequently report feeling "too embarrassed to bring it up." Dramatically impacts relationship quality and survivor well-being.

🧬 Fertility & Reproductive Health Concerns

For younger survivors, the potential impact of cancer and treatment on fertility is a profound concern. Chemotherapy, radiation, and hormonal therapies can impair fertility. Discussions about fertility preservation often happen under extreme time pressure at diagnosis. Post-treatment: uncertainty about safe pregnancy timing, premature ovarian insufficiency, options for family building.

Oncofertility is a subspecialty. Most survivors have poor awareness of their options or where to get guidance post-treatment.

🪞 Body Image & Identity Disruption

Cancer treatment often involves visible, permanent changes to the body: mastectomy, hair loss (permanent in some cases), weight changes, scars, ostomies, prosthetics. The psychological impact of these changes — loss of the "pre-cancer self," disrupted gender identity, intimacy avoidance — is profound and often unaddressed in medical follow-up.

Breast cancer reconstruction decisions are among the most psychologically complex choices a survivor faces, often made under duress and with inadequate support.

👨‍👩‍👧 Family, Caregiver & Genetic Burden

Cancer has a ripple effect: caregivers develop their own psychological distress at rates comparable to patients. For hereditary cancers (BRCA1/2 in breast/ovarian; Lynch syndrome in colorectal), survivors carry the burden of informing family members of their shared genetic risk. Children of survivors have elevated anxiety. Support for the survivor-family system, not just the individual, is a clinical need.

Genetic counseling referral, caregiver support tools, and family communication guidance are largely absent from standard survivorship programs.

The clinical framework determines the product architecture. A platform that treats "cancer survivor distress" as a single construct will fail. These ten domains require distinct content, distinct interventions, and — in some cases — distinct clinical pathways and licensed professionals. Build the taxonomy; route to the right response.

05The Survivorship Cliff — The Most Dangerous Transition

In the HCW mental health parallel, the "acute clinical event" is the trigger moment. In cancer survivorship, the equivalent moment is the end of active treatment — what clinicians call "the survivorship cliff."

"My oncologist said 'you're done with treatment, you're cancer-free, congratulations.' I burst into tears. I wasn't relieved. I was terrified. I'd seen my team every week for months. Now I was just… alone." — Breast cancer survivor, 18 months post-treatment

Why the End of Treatment Is Paradoxically the Most Vulnerable Moment

During active treatment

  • Regular appointments with oncology team (often weekly)
  • Active monitoring — labs, imaging, vital signs
  • Nurse navigator or care coordinator contact
  • Infusion center community and peer connection
  • Clear protocol and role ("I'm fighting cancer")
  • Side effect management is immediate clinical priority
  • Support systems activated by visible illness

After treatment ends — the cliff

  • Appointments drop to every 3–6 months (or annually)
  • Monitoring is surveillance-only — waiting for a problem
  • Care coordination disappears; responsibility shifts to primary care
  • Infusion center community evaporates
  • Loss of identity and purpose: "I'm not a cancer patient anymore but I don't feel normal"
  • Side effects from maintenance therapy emerge without active management
  • Support systems contract as family/friends assume "you're better now"

The Cliff Is When Distress Peaks

Counterintuitively, psychological distress often increases in the months following treatment completion, rather than improving. Multiple studies document this U-shaped or paradoxical distress curve:

  • The protective structure of treatment (regular appointments, clear role, active monitoring) is removed suddenly
  • The "cancer patient" identity, which provided community and clarity, is stripped away without a replacement
  • Fear of recurrence, which was suppressed during the "fight," surfaces fully when fighting is no longer the task
  • Family and friends, who mobilized during acute treatment, begin to disengage — "you're cured, you can move on"
  • The physical side effects of treatment — fatigue, neuropathy, cognitive impairment, endocrine symptoms — often worsen in the first months post-treatment as the body processes the cumulative toxic burden
The product opportunity: own the cliff. The survivorship companion activates at the moment treatment ends — or even in the final weeks of treatment, preparing the survivor for the transition. Unlike clinical tools that end at the last infusion, this platform begins where medicine stops. That is the defensible market position.

Scanxiety: The Recurring Cliff

"Scanxiety" — the intense anxiety experienced in the days and weeks before a surveillance scan (mammogram, CT, PET, colonoscopy, PSA test) — is a recurring trigger that no existing tool specifically addresses. Surveys show 85% of cancer survivors experience significant anxiety around surveillance appointments; many describe the 2–4 weeks before a scan as the worst period of their year, worse than active treatment in some cases.

  • Scans happen every 3–12 months for years to decades post-treatment
  • Each scan reactivates fear of recurrence acutely
  • Results wait periods (often 24–72 hours) are described as psychologically unbearable
  • A platform that detects upcoming scan dates and proactively provides support, coping tools, and peer connection in the scan window addresses a recurring, predictable, high-intensity need

06Why Existing Solutions Fail

Structural failures

  • Generic mental health apps don't speak cancer: Calm, Headspace, and Betterhelp cannot address fear of recurrence, chemo brain, lymphedema management, scanxiety, or the specific psychological dynamics of endocrine therapy. Survivors feel unseen and leave within weeks.
  • Oncology EHRs end at treatment: Epic Beacon (the dominant oncology EHR module) is built for active treatment management — chemotherapy ordering, toxicity tracking, clinical trial management. It has essentially no survivorship infrastructure post-treatment. The EHR relationship ends when the prescription pad closes.
  • Survivorship programs are under-resourced and under-digitized: The 30–40% of cancer centers with formal survivorship programs rely on nurse practitioners and social workers to manually coordinate care with no digital infrastructure. Wait times for survivorship clinic appointments can be months.
  • Navigator programs don't scale: Oncology nurse navigators are among the most valuable humans in the cancer care ecosystem — but they carry caseloads of 50–200 patients and cannot provide the daily support survivors need.
  • Peer support programs are fragmented: CancerCare, the American Cancer Society, and Livestrong all offer peer support, but they are siloed, not personalized to cancer type or treatment history, and not integrated with clinical data.

What the evidence says

Three converging findings from the survivorship literature define what actually works:

  • Timing matters: Psychosocial interventions delivered in the first 6–12 months post-treatment have substantially larger effect sizes than those offered years later. The platform must meet survivors at the cliff, not after they've fallen.
  • Disease-specificity matters: Interventions tailored to breast cancer specifically outperform generic cancer interventions in fear-of-recurrence reduction. A lung cancer survivor and a breast cancer survivor have fundamentally different clinical and psychological needs.
  • Peer support + clinical support is more effective than either alone: Studies of survivorship programs consistently show that combining peer support (survivor-to-survivor connection) with clinical guidance (licensed professional backup) produces better outcomes than either model independently.
  • The SCP alone is insufficient: Receiving a paper or PDF survivorship care plan without active follow-up, personalized guidance, and ongoing support produces minimal behavior change. The plan needs to be living, actionable, and connected to a human or digital touchpoint.
The product opportunity is to be the active, personalized survivorship support layer that activates at the cliff, personalizes to the survivor's specific cancer and treatment history, and persists for the full survivorship journey — not just the first 90 days.

09Survivorship Care Plan (SCP) Engine

The Survivorship Care Plan is the clinical and regulatory anchor of the product. ASCO and the American College of Surgeons Commission on Cancer (CoC) require that accredited cancer programs provide SCPs. Yet the infrastructure for delivering, personalizing, and activating SCPs is almost entirely absent at scale — creating a compliance problem the platform solves.

What a world-class SCP contains

Treatment summary (static)

  • Cancer type, subtype, and stage at diagnosis
  • All surgical procedures and dates
  • Chemotherapy agents, doses, cycles, and dates
  • Radiation fields, doses, and dates
  • Targeted therapies and immunotherapies
  • Hormone therapies: current and planned duration
  • Clinical trials participated in
  • Key pathology (hormone receptor status, HER2, grade)

Living follow-up plan (dynamic)

  • Surveillance schedule: imaging, labs, clinical exams by year post-treatment
  • Late effect monitoring: cardiac (if anthracyclines/trastuzumab), bone density (if AI), neurology (if taxane), lymphedema monitoring (if node dissection)
  • Lifestyle recommendations personalized to treatment: exercise, weight, alcohol, nutrition
  • Medications: current prescriptions, dose, duration, adherence tracking
  • Genetic referral: BRCA testing recommended? Family members to notify?
  • Fertility and reproductive health guidance
  • When to call the oncology team immediately (red flag symptom list)
  • Primary care coordination: what the PCP needs to monitor

The SCP as a distribution and lock-in mechanism

The SCP is not just a clinical document — it is the most powerful onboarding and retention hook the platform has:

  • Institutional distribution: Oncology practices and cancer centers that use the platform can generate ASCO-compliant SCPs for their patients automatically — solving their CoC accreditation compliance problem while populating the platform with new users.
  • Lock-in: Once a survivor's complete treatment history and surveillance calendar lives in the platform, switching cost is extraordinarily high. The SCP is the identity layer for the survivorship journey.
  • PCP bridge: The platform auto-generates a version of the SCP formatted for the primary care physician, closing the hand-off gap that currently leaves PCPs uninformed about cancer history.
  • Interoperability: FHIR-compliant SCP data enables integration with Epic's patient portal (MyChart), hospital health records, and payer care management programs.
The SCP engine is a regulatory compliance product for institutions and a life-organizing platform for survivors simultaneously. No competitor currently offers a digitally delivered, personalized, living SCP at scale. This is the platform's most defensible core feature.

10Peer Support & Survivor Community

Cancer survivors consistently report that connecting with other survivors who have walked the same path is among the most valuable forms of support they receive — often more valued than clinical guidance alone. The peer connection that happens organically in infusion centers, breast cancer support groups, and advocacy organizations is powerful but fragile, local, and inaccessible to the majority of survivors.

Evidence for peer support in survivorship

  • Peer support programs for breast cancer survivors show significant reductions in distress, fear of recurrence, and isolation vs. usual care alone[14]
  • Trained survivor peer advisors improve adherence to endocrine therapy when they proactively address side effect barriers through shared experience[15]
  • Online peer communities for cancer survivors have been associated with improved psychosocial outcomes in multiple studies, with particular benefit for geographically isolated survivors and those with rare cancers[16]
  • Peer mentorship for young breast cancer survivors (under 45) significantly reduces isolation and improves quality of life — a population that is particularly poorly served by traditional support groups dominated by older survivors

How the platform operationalizes peer support

  • Cancer-specific matching: Not "all cancer survivors." Match breast-to-breast, grade III HER2+ to grade III HER2+, mastectomy+reconstruction to mastectomy+reconstruction. Specificity is the value.
  • Treatment stage matching: Connect someone 3 months post-chemo with someone 18 months post-chemo who has been through what they're about to face.
  • Trained survivor advisors: A cohort of volunteer-trained survivor peers, certified in the platform's peer support protocol, available for 1:1 text or video conversations.
  • Community groups by context: Young survivors (under 45), metastatic survivors, BRCA+ survivors, specific drug regimen side effect management, return-to-work, sexuality after cancer.
  • Clinical backstop: Peer support is never the terminal layer. Escalation pathways to licensed professionals are always one tap away.

11Symptom Tracking & Clinical Intelligence

Patient-reported outcome (PRO) collection is the clinical infrastructure layer that transforms the platform from a support app into a clinical tool with institutional and payer value. Real-world PRO data from cancer survivors is among the most sought-after data assets in oncology research, payer quality reporting, and drug post-marketing surveillance.

What the platform tracks (opt-in, survivor-controlled)

Daily wellbeing check-in

  • Energy / fatigue level (PROMIS-Fatigue anchored)
  • Pain (0–10 scale; location if noted)
  • Mood / emotional wellbeing
  • Sleep quality
  • Nausea / appetite
  • Medications taken as prescribed (yes/no + reason if no)

Target: <90 seconds. Adaptive — reduces to 3 questions if stable trend detected over 14 days.

Treatment-specific side effect monitoring

  • Aromatase inhibitor users: joint pain, hot flashes, vaginal symptoms, bone pain
  • Post-surgery: arm mobility, wound healing, lymphedema signs (arm heaviness, swelling)
  • Taxane-treated: neuropathy symptoms (numbness, tingling in hands/feet)
  • HER2+ survivors on trastuzumab: cardiac symptom watch (shortness of breath)
  • Radiation: skin changes, fatigue patterns, field-specific symptoms

Psychosocial PRO instruments

  • PHQ-2 / PHQ-9 (depression screening)
  • GAD-7 (anxiety)
  • FCR brief scale (Fear of Recurrence)
  • PROMIS Global Health
  • FACIT-Fatigue
  • Financial distress single-item screen
  • Sleep Quality Index (abbreviated)

Deployed adaptively: triggered by clinical indication, not on fixed calendar schedule to avoid alert fatigue.

Clinical intelligence layer

  • Trend detection: If fatigue scores increase consistently over 14 days, or if a survivor reports 5+ missed endocrine therapy doses in a month, the system flags this and offers an intervention — not just a reminder.
  • Pre-appointment summary: Survivor generates a 3–6 month symptom trend summary to bring to the oncology or primary care appointment. Time-pressed oncologists have reported this dramatically improves the quality of survivorship conversations in pilot programs.
  • Red flag escalation: Certain symptom combinations trigger immediate clinical escalation prompts: new arm swelling (lymphedema), chest pain + shortness of breath (cardiac in anthracycline-treated), new breast lump, unexplained bone pain. The platform never diagnoses — it flags and routes.
  • Aggregate data for research: De-identified, aggregated PRO data from consenting survivors is a high-value research asset. Oncology research institutions, pharmaceutical companies (for post-marketing surveillance), and payers are active buyers of real-world survivorship outcome data.
The PRO data layer is the platform's secondary revenue engine and long-term data moat. A survivor who uses the platform for 5 years generates a longitudinal health record that no clinical trial can replicate — because it captures daily life experience, not quarterly clinic snapshots. This data, aggregated and de-identified with robust consent, becomes a premium asset for oncology research.

12Mental Health & Psychological Support Module

Cancer-specific mental health needs require cancer-specific interventions. The fear-of-recurrence framework that works for a breast cancer survivor is built on a fundamentally different theoretical model than generalized anxiety disorder treatment. The platform must deliver evidence-based, oncology-specific psychological support — not generic CBT rebranded with a cancer ribbon.

Evidence-based interventions for cancer-specific psychological needs

Fear of Recurrence (FCR)

Cognitive Existential Therapy (CET) and Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)-based interventions for FCR have the strongest evidence base. Key elements:

  • Psychoeducation: normalizing FCR as a near-universal survivor experience, not a pathology
  • Cognitive restructuring: challenging catastrophizing thought patterns about symptoms and scans
  • Acceptance work: moving from control-seeking to tolerance of uncertainty (the core existential challenge of survivorship)
  • Trigger identification and coping plan: scanxiety pre-planning, symptom-check behavior management
  • Values-based action: redirecting attention from cancer threat to valued life activities

RCT evidence for app-delivered CBT for FCR in breast cancer survivors shows significant reductions in FCR severity at 3 and 6 months.[17]

Cancer-Related Cognitive Impairment ("Chemo Brain")

Cognitive rehabilitation approaches adapted for cancer survivors:

  • Psychoeducation: Validating CRCI as real and documented — many survivors are told "it's just stress" for years
  • Compensatory strategy training: Memory aids, calendars, chunking, reducing cognitive load
  • Attention training: Cognitive exercise protocols validated in cancer populations
  • Sleep and fatigue management: CRCI is worsened by fatigue and poor sleep — treating these improves cognitive function
  • Work accommodation planning: Return-to-work support with documentation tools for cognitive accommodations

Scanxiety Management Protocol

A structured pre-scan support sequence, activating 2 weeks before each surveillance appointment:

  • Day 14–8 before scan: Psychoeducation on scanxiety; normalization; CBT introductory module
  • Day 7 before scan: Peer connection with survivor who had same scan recently
  • Day 3–1 before scan: Anxiety regulation tools; grounding exercises; pre-prepared coping plan for results
  • Results wait period: Active distraction coping tools; peer check-in option; results anxiety journaling
  • Post-results: Celebration (good results) OR distress support pathway (concerning results) + clinical escalation if needed

Grief, Loss & Identity

Cancer survivorship involves a series of losses that are rarely acknowledged in medical settings: loss of the pre-cancer body, loss of certainty about the future, potential loss of fertility, loss of the "cancer patient" identity when treatment ends, loss of the infusion center community. Grief-informed approaches:

  • Narrative therapy: writing and sharing the survivor story
  • Post-traumatic growth framing: meaning-making without toxic positivity
  • Identity reconstruction exercises: who am I now, what matters, what's next
  • Body image work: specific to mastectomy, hair loss, weight changes, scars
  • Peer connection: shared narrative with other survivors navigating identity

13Sexual Health, Intimacy & Fertility

Sexual health disruption is among the most prevalent and least addressed survivorship issues in oncology. Studies consistently show it affects 40–80% of cancer survivors depending on cancer type and treatment, yet fewer than 30% of oncology practices proactively screen for or address sexual health concerns. Survivors report feeling "too embarrassed to bring it up," and clinicians — equally uncomfortable — rarely initiate the conversation.

Breast cancer: specific sexual health burden

  • Aromatase inhibitors/tamoxifen: Vaginal dryness, dyspareunia (painful intercourse), reduced libido, and vulvovaginal atrophy affect 50–70% of women on endocrine therapy — and are among the top reasons for discontinuation, with direct recurrence risk consequences[18]
  • Premature menopause: Chemotherapy-induced ovarian failure causes abrupt menopause in pre-menopausal women — without the gradual transition that provides natural adaptation time
  • Mastectomy and body image: Profound changes in self-perception, partner relationship dynamics, and willingness to engage sexually
  • Fatigue: Cancer-related fatigue — often most severe in the post-treatment period — is a major inhibitor of sexual desire and activity
  • Relationship impact: Partners frequently report feeling helpless, afraid of hurting the survivor, or uncertain about how to re-engage intimately after treatment

Platform approach: taboo → normalized, routable

  • Proactive screening: Brief validated sexual health screen at 3 and 6 months post-treatment — asked by the app, not the oncologist, removing the interpersonal barrier
  • Psychoeducation: Normalize that sexual health changes are medical side effects of treatment — not personal failures or permanent states
  • Evidence-based self-management: Vaginal moisturizers, lubricants, pelvic floor exercises — content created with gynecologic oncology specialists
  • Partner resources: Guided content for partners navigating intimacy after cancer treatment
  • Routing to specialists: Certified sexual health counselors, gynecologic oncology NPs, pelvic floor physical therapists — a specialist network that most survivors don't know exists
  • Peer community: Private groups specifically for survivors navigating intimacy — the most-used and highest-rated feature in comparable digital support programs

Fertility: the young survivor dimension

For premenopausal breast cancer survivors and young adult cancer survivors broadly, fertility is a defining survivorship concern that the medical system handles poorly:

  • Fertility preservation discussions at diagnosis happen under extreme time pressure — often the day of or after diagnosis — when cognitive and emotional capacity to process options is minimal
  • Post-treatment: survivors face uncertainty about when (or whether) it is safe to attempt pregnancy, how hormone changes affect fertility, and what options exist for family building after treatment-induced ovarian insufficiency
  • ASCO guidelines recommend all patients receive fertility preservation counseling before gonadotoxic treatment — yet studies show this happens in only 30–40% of eligible cases[19]
  • The platform provides: post-treatment fertility guidance by treatment type, oncofertility specialist referral network, peer communities for survivors navigating family-building decisions, and evidence-based information on pregnancy after breast cancer (which is safe for most HR- survivors with appropriate waiting periods)
Sexual health and fertility are the topics survivors most want to talk about and most rarely get to. A platform that normalizes these conversations and routes to appropriate specialists will have disproportionately high engagement and loyalty in these modules — because survivors have nowhere else to go.

The cancer survivor population growth curve — a secular tailwind

Unlike most healthcare markets, the cancer survivorship market is structurally growing faster than the underlying disease prevalence, driven by:

  • Improving survival rates: 5-year survival has improved for virtually every major cancer type over the past two decades. More diagnoses → more survivors → larger addressable population.
  • Earlier detection: Low-dose CT lung cancer screening, multi-cancer early detection (MCED) blood tests (Galleri, etc.) will dramatically increase early-stage diagnoses — most of which are highly survivable.
  • Aging population: Cancer incidence peaks in the 60s–70s. The U.S. population is aging. Cancer survivorship demographics will expand as a result.
  • New therapies extending survival in previously fatal cancers: Immunotherapy and targeted therapies have converted some metastatic cancers from death sentences to chronic diseases — creating a new population of "long-term metastatic survivors" with unique needs.

20Trust, Privacy & Compliance Architecture

Trust design requirements

  • Cancer diagnosis is among the most sensitive personal health information — protection must be architectural, not just policy
  • Employers never receive individual survivor data — this is non-negotiable for GINA (Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act) compliance and survivor trust
  • Payers never receive individual PRO data without survivor's granular consent — even in payer-funded deployments
  • Pharma partners never receive identifiable individual data — aggregate, de-identified adherence analytics only, with robust consent
  • Genetic information (BRCA status, Lynch syndrome results) receives heightened protection — separate consent tier, separate storage, explicit disclosure controls
  • Survivor controls all clinical sharing: generates appointment summaries explicitly, per appointment
  • Community content is pseudonymous — real first name only, cancer type and stage visible only to cancer-type matched peers
  • Research data use requires separate, specific, revocable consent — not bundled into Terms of Service

Compliance watchouts

  • HIPAA: Cancer survivorship platform handles PHI (Protected Health Information) by definition. Full HIPAA compliance, BAAs with all institutional partners, minimum-necessary data principles required[26]
  • GINA: Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act prohibits employers from using genetic information (including BRCA status) in employment decisions. Employer-funded deployments require strict information barriers[27]
  • ADA: Cancer is a protected disability under the ADA; survivors using the platform to document work accommodation needs must be protected from employer surveillance of accommodation requests
  • FDA CDS guidance: Symptom escalation features and PRO-based clinical recommendations must be carefully scoped to avoid clinical decision support regulatory classification
  • FTC health breach notification rule: App-based health data not covered by HIPAA is subject to FTC rules; hybrid architecture requires careful legal structure
  • State privacy laws: California CCPA/CPRA, Washington My Health MY Data Act, and other state health data laws add requirements beyond HIPAA for non-covered entities
The GINA architecture problem: If an employer pays for the platform as a benefit, the platform must be structured so that the employer cannot identify which employees used it for genetic cancer risk information. The information wall between benefit administrator and individual usage must be technically enforced, not just contractually promised. This is a competitive advantage — smaller players cannot build this architecture correctly.

24Validation & Pilot Design

3-month DTC pilot (proof of engagement)

  • Target: 500–1,000 breast cancer survivors recruited through Living Beyond Breast Cancer and Young Survival Coalition partnerships
  • Primary metrics: 30-day retention rate, weekly active usage rate, SCP engagement depth, peer connection conversion rate
  • Secondary metrics: FCR brief scale change at 6 and 12 weeks vs. baseline; PHQ-2 trend; self-reported endocrine therapy adherence; NPS (survivor satisfaction)
  • Qualitative interviews at 4 and 12 weeks: what's working, what feels missing, what's the most valuable feature
  • Financial toxicity screen administered at onboarding and 12 weeks

12-month institutional pilot (proof of ROI)

  • Target: 2–3 NCI-designated cancer centers; 300–800 breast cancer survivors entering survivorship per site per year
  • Hard outcome targets: Endocrine therapy adherence at 12 months vs. matched historical control; PRO score improvement vs. usual care; ED utilization rate in platform vs. non-platform cohort
  • Institutional metrics: SCP delivery compliance rate (CoC metric); time-to-SCP delivery; survivorship care coordination handoffs to primary care completed
  • Publish findings in Journal of Clinical Oncology or JCO Oncology Practice — peer-reviewed publication creates clinical credibility and competitive moat simultaneously
  • Economic analysis: cost per adherent survivor-year; ED avoidance value; productivity return-to-work metric for employer channel

Key assumptions to validate in the first 60 days

  • Survivors will self-onboard treatment history accurately and completely enough to generate useful, personalized SCPs — or FHIR import is needed from Day 1
  • Scanxiety protocol generates measurably higher engagement than any other platform feature — validating the pre-scan trigger as the most important activation hook
  • Peer connection within the first 2 weeks drives retention through 30 days — the primary churn intervention
  • Endocrine therapy side effect management content drives measurably higher self-reported adherence vs. no support — the primary pharma partnership value proposition
  • Survivors are willing to share PRO data with their oncology team (to validate the pre-appointment summary feature's clinical adoption)
